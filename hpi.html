A human-powered interface

The Email Sender


As part of the Advanced Project course of the Interactive Media Technology master's program, different theories and methods related to the fields of human-powered interaction, online behavior and sustainability have been explored. As a result of such exploration, a human-powered interactive device capable of sending emails has been developed: The Email Sender.

The Email Sender consists of two main parts: the crank and the feedback interface. By cranking, the motor attached to the crank powers the whole system. The feedback interface serves both as a guide and as an input since it shows the status of the process through the status indicators (flipdots), and also gathers the kind of email the user wants to send by pressing one of the four buttons: to send an mail with a simple sentence, an email with a text file, an email with an image or an email with an audio file.

Three strong concepts have been studied in this project. The human energy needed to power a device which performs basic daily tasks, the carbon footprint generated by the specific daily task, in our case, sending an email, and the interaction with a human-powered interface.

Energy is often described as intangible and in need of visualisation. We believe that one of the achievements made with the Email Sender is that, after using the device, the user is able to materialise "how much energy" he or she needs to be able to send an email. Thus, making the energy tangible, the user gains a bigger perspective about energy requirements and it creates a concern on the energy needed to perform basic daily tasks that we usually take for granted.

The carbon footprint generated by an email is a topic by its own, but we see a strong correlation between the human energy needed to send and email and its carbon footprint since both are usually unknown adverse factors of simple daily tasks. Sending an email with an attachment on it generates, for instance in the case of the voice clip used for the Email Sender, a carbon footprint of approximately 3 g CO2e. We found that in many cases, email users do not even know that sending emails highly pollute.

The interaction part of the project gave us room for improvement of the prototype. Feedback from users taught us that, for instance, pressing the button to select the email while cranking is not an optimal function, or that using pedals to power the system while leaving the hands free to interact with the interface could have been a better approach.

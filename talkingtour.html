Walk, tour and sigthseeing a city through sound navigation

The Talking Tour App

Having to pay attention to mobile phones, tablets or any kind of screen interfaces distracts our attention from what surrounds us. Same situation happens when a tourist is exploring a new and unknown city and he is forced to, somehow, follow visual instructions from a personal device. It is likely that this person who is walking and sightseeing in a new place wants to contemplate the surroundings and observe any monuments, architectural buildings or places of interest without any intrusive distraction.

For the Media Technology course in the Interactive Technology Master's at KTH, a disruptive way of navigation has been proposed, the so-called 'sound navigation'. This concept encompasses the experience of following navigation instructions through the use of different sounds and records so that our sight is free of distractions. The main idea would be that the app lands on a discovery screen from where the user should point with its phone to the direction he would like to walk. Making use of the geomagnetic field sensor and the accelerometer sensor on the phone, the app would be able to show the highlighted places within certain range. Once the user knows where he wants to go, he would be guided just by the use of sound until he or she reaches the desired point. Once in front of the place, a story would be reproduced automatically telling historical or interesting facts of the place. The navigation itself would be tracked by using GPS.

The proposed solution consists of a series of sketches of how the final prototype of the app would look like: the 'discover' phase previously explained and a personal section where the user could save and manage the visited places’ tracks.

Project members: Diego Martín, Emma Andersson, Jonathan Ramírez Mendoza and Elena González Rabal.

The traditional memory game adapted to haptics

Haptic Memory Game

The Haptic Memory game is the project resulting from the course Haptic, Tactile, and Tangible Interaction of the Master’s Interactive Media Technology at KTH. This project aims to answer the research question "How difficult is shape recognition with haptic devices?" by the use of a memory game adapted to a haptic device. In order to understand the game, two different concepts are explained: haptic perception and haptic device.

Haptic perception is defined as the perception in which both the cutaneous sense and the kinesthesis convey significant information about distal objects and events. Moreover, haptic devices are devices designed in a way that they apply resistance and forces via motors in the joints of a robotic arm. The resistance is applied based on the surface of a graphical object when the proxy of the device is virtually placed on it.

Haptic devices' applications could vary from an astronaut teleoperating a robot outside the International Space Station to a surgeon using a robot to perform a delicate procedure at a scale not achievable with the human hand or in training where the trainee person learns by trial and error method and this cannot be done directly in real scenarios such us humans or factories. For the previous situations, the help of a haptic device is really valuable since they give to the person operating them the sense of “telepresence”. The operator basically feels as if he or she is directly manipulating the environment with their own hands.

For the Haptic Memory Game project a Phantom Omni has been used. It is a commercial, portable haptic device with six Degrees of Freedom. The workspace of the Phantom Omni is 16 cm x 12 cm x 7 cm (W x H x D) and can be provide force feedback up to 3.3 N.

The game itself has been developed as an adaptation to the traditional memory game where the player/s have to match the cards by peers based on the image drawn on them. In the Haptic Memory Game, the user has to match the shapes hidden in boxes by hapticly feeling them. The game has been developed using the open source haptic framework Chai3D and the shapes chosen for the study to be recognized are the sphere, the cylinder, the cube and the torus.

When the player achieves to haptically match two shapes, the boxes hiding the shapes are automatically deleted as an analogy for the real game where the player leaves the cards right-side up. The game is over when the user achieves to match all the shapes with their own pairs.

At the end of the game, each participant had to complete a questionnaire whose solutions show that the sphere is the easier shape to recognize, then the cylinder followed by the cube and finally the torus.

Project members: Judith Sirera I Pulido, Tom Axblad, and Elena González Rabal.

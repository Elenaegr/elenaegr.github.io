Navigate your way through haptic vibration

Haptic handlebar


The haptic handlebar is a proof of concept to study the idea of navigation through haptic vibration installed on the bike/scooter handlebar. The concept is simple; if the right handle vibrates, you should turn to right. If the left handle vibrates, you should turn to left.

The idea behind this concept is to avoid visual interfaces in which the user needs to stop paying attention to the road in order to look at the navigation screen. By installing vibro motors on the handlebar, the user receives the passive indications, without distractions, since they already have their hands on the system.

With the aim of building a working prototype, different motors have been tested. Mainly ERM and LRA motors were chosen and embedded to the handlebar for the testing.

The final prototype was tested on real users. A Wizard of Oz technique was implemented to mock the navigation instructions. The system was composed by a receiver and a sender, being the receiver the user testing the system and getting the vibration feedback, and the sender the user behind acting as the navigation system and sending the turning instructions to the testing user.

After carrying out the test on different subjects, some conclusions were withdrawn from the results. Vibrations from the road are big interferences to consider when designing a system like the one proposed. In addition, in cold weather environments, if the user wears gloves or any other protection, they would act as an interference diminishing the feedback and, consequently, the effectiveness of the system. Finally, in a situation where more than one turning points are very close to each other, the system does not help to discern which one the user should take, and, therefore, the system would be helpful to orientate more than to navigate to a final location.

This project was developed during my internship as interaction designer at Propeller under the supervision of Markus Stridsberg and Kristofer Vahlström.
